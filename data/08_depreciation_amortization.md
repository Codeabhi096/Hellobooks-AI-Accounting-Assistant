# Depreciation & Amortization

## Overview
Depreciation and amortization are accounting methods used to allocate the cost of a long-term asset over its useful life. Rather than recording the full cost as an expense in the year of purchase, the cost is spread across multiple periods to match the revenue the asset helps generate.

- **Depreciation** — applies to **tangible assets** (physical items)
- **Amortization** — applies to **intangible assets** (non-physical items)

---

## Why It Matters
- Matches expenses to the periods in which assets provide value (Matching Principle)
- Reduces taxable income each year
- Reflects the true declining value of assets on the Balance Sheet
- Affects both the P&L (expense) and Balance Sheet (accumulated depreciation)

---

## Common Depreciation Methods

### 1. Straight-Line Depreciation
The most common method. Equal expense is recorded each year.

> **Annual Depreciation = (Cost − Salvage Value) / Useful Life**

**Example:**
- Equipment cost: $50,000
- Salvage value: $5,000
- Useful life: 5 years
- Annual depreciation: ($50,000 − $5,000) / 5 = **$9,000/year**

---

### 2. Declining Balance (Accelerated)
Higher depreciation in early years, declining over time.

> **Depreciation = Book Value × Depreciation Rate**

Useful for assets that lose value quickly (e.g., technology, vehicles).

---

### 3. Units of Production
Depreciation based on actual usage, not time.

> **Depreciation = (Cost − Salvage Value) / Total Units × Units Produced**

Best for machinery or vehicles where wear depends on use.

---

### 4. Sum of the Years' Digits (SYD)
An accelerated method that front-loads depreciation.

---

## Depreciation on the Financial Statements

| Statement | Impact |
|---|---|
| **P&L** | Depreciation appears as an operating expense |
| **Balance Sheet** | Accumulated depreciation reduces the asset's book value |
| **Cash Flow** | Added back to net income in operating activities (non-cash) |

---

## Book Value vs. Market Value

> **Book Value = Original Cost − Accumulated Depreciation**

Book value is the accounting value of an asset, not necessarily its market value. An asset may be fully depreciated (book value = $0) but still in use or have market value.

---

## Amortization of Intangibles

Common intangible assets that are amortized:

| Asset | Typical Life |
|---|---|
| Patents | 20 years (or remaining legal life) |
| Software | 3–5 years |
| Customer lists | 5–10 years |
| Trademarks | Indefinite (not amortized — tested for impairment) |
| Goodwill | Indefinite (not amortized — tested for impairment) |

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Useful Life** | Expected period the asset will be used |
| **Salvage Value** | Estimated residual value at end of useful life |
| **Accumulated Depreciation** | Total depreciation recorded to date |
| **Net Book Value** | Cost minus accumulated depreciation |
| **Impairment** | When an asset's value drops below book value unexpectedly |
| **Capital Expenditure (CapEx)** | The purchase of a depreciable asset |
