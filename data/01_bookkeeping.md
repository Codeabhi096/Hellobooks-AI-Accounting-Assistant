# Bookkeeping

## Overview
Bookkeeping is the systematic process of recording, organizing, and maintaining all financial transactions of a business. It forms the foundation of accounting and ensures that financial data is accurate, up-to-date, and ready for reporting.

---

## Key Concepts

### Double-Entry Bookkeeping
Every financial transaction affects at least two accounts — one is debited and one is credited. The fundamental equation must always remain balanced:

> **Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

**Example:**
- A business buys office supplies worth $500 on credit.
  - Debit: Office Supplies (Asset) $500
  - Credit: Accounts Payable (Liability) $500

### Single-Entry Bookkeeping
A simpler system used by very small businesses. Only one entry is made per transaction — similar to a personal checkbook. Less accurate and not suitable for businesses with complex finances.

---

## The Bookkeeping Cycle

1. **Identify transactions** — Sales, purchases, payments, receipts
2. **Record in journals** — Chronological listing of all transactions (General Journal)
3. **Post to ledgers** — Transfer journal entries to individual account ledgers (General Ledger)
4. **Prepare a trial balance** — Verify that debits equal credits
5. **Adjust entries** — Account for accruals, deferrals, depreciation
6. **Close the books** — Reset temporary accounts at end of period

---

## Common Accounts in Bookkeeping

| Account Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Assets | Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Equipment |
| Liabilities | Accounts Payable, Loans Payable, Accrued Expenses |
| Equity | Owner's Capital, Retained Earnings |
| Revenue | Sales Revenue, Service Income |
| Expenses | Rent, Salaries, Utilities, Cost of Goods Sold |

---

## Key Documents
- **Sales receipts** — Record of money received
- **Purchase invoices** — Record of money owed to suppliers
- **Bank statements** — Used for reconciliation
- **Payroll records** — Employee wage and tax details
- **Expense receipts** — Evidence of business expenses

---

## Best Practices
- Record transactions promptly and consistently
- Reconcile bank accounts monthly
- Keep personal and business finances separate
- Retain all source documents for audit purposes
- Use accounting software (e.g., QuickBooks, Xero, Wave) to reduce errors
