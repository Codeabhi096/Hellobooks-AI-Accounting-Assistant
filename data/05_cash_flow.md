# Cash Flow Statement

## Overview
The Cash Flow Statement is a financial report that tracks the actual movement of cash **into and out of** a business over a specific period. Unlike the P&L (which uses accrual accounting), the cash flow statement focuses solely on real cash transactions.

> **A profitable business can still fail if it runs out of cash.**

---

## Purpose
- Monitor the business's ability to pay bills and obligations
- Show where cash is being generated and spent
- Identify periods of cash shortage before they become critical
- Complement the P&L and Balance Sheet for a full financial picture
- Used by lenders and investors to assess financial sustainability

---

## The Three Sections of Cash Flow

### 1. Operating Activities
Cash generated or used by the core business operations.

**Cash Inflows:**
- Collections from customers
- Interest received
- Dividends received (in some frameworks)

**Cash Outflows:**
- Payments to suppliers
- Employee salaries and wages
- Rent, utilities, insurance
- Tax payments
- Interest paid

> **This is the most important section** — positive operating cash flow means the business generates cash from its core activities.

---

### 2. Investing Activities
Cash used for long-term investments and asset purchases/sales.

**Cash Inflows:**
- Proceeds from sale of equipment or property
- Sale of investments (stocks, bonds)
- Loan repayments received

**Cash Outflows:**
- Purchase of property, plant & equipment (CapEx)
- Acquisition of other businesses
- Purchase of investments

> Negative investing cash flow is often a sign of **growth** (investing in assets).

---

### 3. Financing Activities
Cash related to funding the business through debt or equity.

**Cash Inflows:**
- Proceeds from bank loans or bonds
- Issuance of new shares

**Cash Outflows:**
- Repayment of loans
- Payment of dividends
- Share buybacks

---

## Cash Flow Formula

> **Ending Cash = Beginning Cash + Net Cash from Operating + Investing + Financing Activities**

## Direct vs. Indirect Method

| | Direct Method | Indirect Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Approach** | Lists all actual cash receipts and payments | Starts with net profit, adjusts for non-cash items |
| **Detail** | More transparent | More common in practice |
| **Complexity** | More complex to prepare | Easier to prepare |

---

## Key Cash Flow Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Free Cash Flow (FCF) | Operating Cash Flow − CapEx | Cash available after investments |
| Operating Cash Flow Ratio | Operating CF / Current Liabilities | Short-term debt coverage |
| Cash Flow Margin | Operating CF / Revenue × 100 | Cash efficiency of revenue |

---

## Common Cash Flow Problems
- **Overtrading** — Growing too fast without sufficient cash reserves
- **Late receivables** — Customers paying slowly while bills come due
- **Seasonal fluctuations** — Revenue peaks and troughs causing cash gaps
- **Over-investment** — Spending heavily on assets, starving operations of cash

---

## Best Practices
- Forecast cash flow at least 3 months ahead
- Chase overdue invoices proactively
- Maintain a cash reserve (emergency fund)
- Negotiate favourable payment terms with suppliers
- Use a rolling 13-week cash flow model for operational control
